\## 1. Executive Summary



Vinamilk enters FY2025 as a fundamentally sound, cash-generative consumer staples business — but one that is chronically under-leveraged and increasingly inefficient with its capital base. Three findings dominate.



First, economic value creation is real and substantial: EVA of 6,404.32 VND bn confirms the business earns well above its 9% cost of capital, and the market has priced this correctly at 3.95× book. Second, profitability is strong in absolute terms — ROE of 26.02% sits at the upper bound of Vietnamese consumer staples peers (15–25%) — but the Du Pont decomposition reveals that this return is driven almost entirely by operating margin and asset turnover, not leverage. With a leverage ratio of just 1.55×, Vinamilk is leaving financial amplification entirely unused. Third, the capital allocation posture is contradictory: the company simultaneously holds cash exceeding its entire current liabilities (cash ratio 1.25×) while paying out dividends (11,264.48 VND bn) that exceed FY2025 net income (9,413.59 VND bn). The combination of idle cash, near-zero debt, and a payout exceeding earnings is the single most consequential strategic question this analysis surfaces.

\## 2. Company \& Data Overview



Vietnam Dairy Products Joint Stock Company (Vinamilk), ticker VNM on the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE), is Vietnam's dominant dairy producer and one of the largest listed consumer staples companies in Southeast Asia. This analysis covers FY2025 (1 January – 31 December 2025) with FY2024 comparatives.



All financial data sourced from Vinamilk's FY2025 audited statements prepared under Vietnamese Accounting Standards (VAS), retrieved via Vietstock Finance. All monetary figures are in VND billions (Tỷ VND) throughout.



\*\*VAS depreciation note:\*\* Under VAS, depreciation is embedded within cost of goods sold and SG\&A — no stand-alone depreciation line appears on the Income Statement (`INC\_depreciation` = 0.00). The true depreciation add-back of 1,233.18 VND bn is sourced exclusively from the Cash Flow Statement (`CASH\_depreciation`) and applied only in the cash coverage ratio.

\## 3. Performance



\*\*Market Value Added (MVA): 101,575.98 VND bn\*\*



Vinamilk's market capitalisation of 136,059 VND bn against book equity of 34,483.03 VND bn implies the market assigns 101,575.98 VND bn of value above the accounting balance sheet. This is not a valuation curiosity — it reflects the market's pricing of Vinamilk's brand equity, distribution network, and pricing power that VAS accounting does not capture on the balance sheet. At nearly 3× the book value of equity, it also sets a high bar: the business must continue generating EVA to justify the premium.



\*\*Economic Value Added (EVA): 6,404.32 VND bn\*\*



NOPAT of 9,674.23 VND bn minus the 9% capital charge on start-of-year capitalisation (0.09 × 36,332.31 = 3,269.91 VND bn) yields EVA of 6,404.32 VND bn. Vinamilk earns approximately 2.96× its cost of capital on its invested base — a result in the top tier of Vietnamese listed manufacturers. The EVA is structurally supported by high operating margins rather than financial engineering; it should prove durable as long as Vinamilk maintains its pricing position in the domestic dairy market.



\*\*Market-to-Book: 3.95×\*\*



At 3.95×, Vinamilk trades toward the upper end of the Vietnamese dairy and FMCG peer range (2–5×). This is consistent with EVA-positive performance, but it also means any deterioration in operating margins or asset efficiency will be disproportionately punished by the market. The premium is justified today; it creates execution risk tomorrow.



\*\*Cross-category link:\*\* The MVA premium is directly traceable to the 26.02% ROE and the 15.20% operating profit margin surfaced in the Du Pont decomposition. If either deteriorates materially — particularly the margin, which is discussed in Section 5 — the premium re-rating risk is significant.

\*\*Asset turnover of 1.16×\*\* is respectable for a manufacturing-intensive dairy business with 34,581.52 VND bn in gross PP\&E. Vinamilk generates VND 1.16 of revenue per VND 1.00 of asset base — but this ratio is declining directionally: total assets fell from 55,049.06 to 53,312.36 VND bn while revenue of 63,645.89 VND bn is the denominator driver. If revenue growth stalls while the PP\&E base expands (gross PP\&E grew 1,868.47 VND bn in FY2025), asset turnover will compress in future periods.



\*\*Average collection period of 35.75 days\*\* is tight and appropriate for a domestic FMCG business selling primarily to distributors and modern trade channels. Receivables turnover of 10.21× confirms that Vinamilk's customers pay promptly; this is a low-risk receivables book. There is no remediation required here.



\*\*Days in inventory of 58.49 days\*\* warrants monitoring. For a dairy company with perishable raw materials, a 58-day inventory cycle is elevated. Inventory grew from 5,686.84 to 6,839.28 VND bn year-over-year (+1,152.44 VND bn, +20.3%), faster than revenue growth. This could reflect deliberate safety-stock build ahead of input price increases, but it consumes working capital and depresses asset turnover. If sustained, it will erode EVA.



\*\*Operating profit margin of 15.20%\*\* is healthy for dairy manufacturing, particularly given that VAS-embedded depreciation within COGS (35,487.58 VND bn) and SGA (15,131.68 VND bn) is already fully absorbed before arriving at this figure. The COGS-to-sales ratio of 55.76% and SGA-to-sales ratio of 23.77% leave limited margin for cost-driven expansion; future margin improvement must come from pricing power or volume leverage, not from cost reduction alone.



\*\*Du Pont link:\*\* Asset turnover (1.16×) and operating profit margin (15.20%) multiply directly to produce ROA of 17.57% — algebraically verified in Section 8. The binding constraint on ROA is the operating margin, not the asset base; a 1 percentage point improvement in margin generates more ROA lift than an equivalent improvement in asset turnover at current levels.

\## 6. Leverage

| Long-term debt-to-equity | 0.00x |

| Total debt ratio | 35.32% |

| Times interest earned (TIE) | 32.73x |

| Cash coverage ratio | 36.52x |

| Debt burden | 0.97x |

Vinamilk's capital structure is, in practical terms, entirely equity-financed. Long-term debt of 62.91 VND bn against equity of 34,483.03 VND bn produces a long-term debt-to-equity ratio of 0.002× — negligible by any standard. The 35.32% total debt ratio reflects current operating liabilities (trade payables, short-term debt due for repayment at 9,393.74 VND bn) rather than any structural financial leverage decision.



\*\*TIE of 32.73× and cash coverage of 36.52×\*\* are not coverage metrics in any meaningful risk sense — they quantify unused debt capacity. A business with TIE above 30× and essentially no long-term debt has the balance sheet capacity to take on material leverage without approaching distress territory. The cash coverage ratio uses `CASH\_depreciation` = 1,233.18 VND bn per VAS treatment; had the Income Statement depreciation line been used (0.00 under VAS), the result would have been identical to TIE — a model error the spec explicitly prevents.



\*\*Debt burden of 0.97×\*\* indicates that net income (9,413.59 VND bn) is 97.3% of NOPAT (9,674.23 VND bn). The 2.7% gap represents the net after-tax cost of interest: interest expense of 325.80 VND bn × (1 − 0.20) = 260.64 VND bn. With virtually no long-term debt, the income statement is essentially free of financing drag.



\*\*Du Pont link:\*\* The leverage ratio of 1.55× is the Du Pont multiplier that scales operating returns up to equity returns. Because leverage is so low, ROE tracks ROA closely. Section 8 quantifies this precisely.

\## 7. Liquidity



| Ratio | Value |

|-------|------:|

| Net working capital to assets | 33.27% |

| Current ratio | 1.96x |

| Quick ratio | 1.58x |

| Cash ratio | 1.25x |



Vinamilk's liquidity position is exceptional to the point of raising capital allocation questions. A \*\*current ratio of 1.96×\*\* and \*\*quick ratio of 1.58×\*\* indicate comfortable short-term solvency. The \*\*cash ratio of 1.25×\*\* — meaning cash and marketable securities of 23,149.74 VND bn cover all current liabilities of 18,520.29 VND bn by a factor of 1.25× — is the figure that demands attention.



This is not a conservative liquidity buffer; it is an oversized cash position that earns below the 9% WACC and therefore destroys EVA at scale. The tension is sharpest when placed alongside the dividend decision: Vinamilk paid out 11,264.48 VND bn in dividends in FY2025, exceeding net income of 9,413.59 VND bn by 1,850.89 VND bn and drawing down retained earnings. The business is simultaneously holding excess cash and paying out capital it cannot cover from current-year earnings. This incoherence — maintain a cash hoard, but distribute beyond earnings — is the central capital policy contradiction that management must resolve.



\*\*Cross-category link:\*\* Cash earning below WACC is the primary suppressor of EVA. If 5,000 VND bn of the excess cash balance were deployed into operating investments returning at the current ROA of 17.57%, EVA would increase by approximately 879 VND bn annually (5,000 × (17.57% − 9%)).

\## 8. Du Pont Decomposition



\### Component Values



| Component | Named Range | Formula | Value |

|-----------|-------------|---------|------:|

| Leverage | `RATIO\_leverage` | `BAL\_assets\_total\_2025 / BAL\_equity\_shareholders\_2025` | 1.55x |

| Asset turnover | `RATIO\_asset\_turnover` | `INC\_sales / BAL\_assets\_total\_2024` | 1.16x |

| Operating profit margin | `RATIO\_operating\_profit\_margin` | `currentYear\_after\_tax\_operating\_income / INC\_sales` | 15.20% |

| Debt burden | `RATIO\_debt\_burden` | `INC\_net / currentYear\_after\_tax\_operating\_income` | 0.97x |



\### Du Pont ROE



\*\*ROE (Du Pont) = 1.55 × 1.16 × 15.20% × 0.97 = 26.44%\*\*



Full computation:

\- Leverage × Asset turnover: 1.5460 × 1.1562 = 1.7875

\- × Operating profit margin: 1.7875 × 0.1520 = 0.2717 (= Du Pont ROA: 27.17% pre-debt-burden)

\- × Debt burden: 0.2717 × 0.9731 = 0.2644



\*\*Du Pont ROA (cross-check): 1.1562 × 0.1520 = 17.57%\*\*

Direct ROA = 9,674.23 / 55,049.062 = \*\*17.57%\*\* ✓ Exact match — Validation Rule V3 satisfied.



\### Primary Driver Identification



The dominant driver of Vinamilk's 26.44% Du Pont ROE is operating profit margin at 15.20%. To illustrate the contribution hierarchy: if leverage were 1.0× (pure equity, no asset-equity gap), ROE would equal ROA × debt burden = 17.57% × 0.97 = 17.04%. The leverage ratio of 1.55× amplifies this by 0.55× of the ROA base, contributing approximately 9.40 percentage points to the final ROE. Operating margin is the foundation; leverage is a modest amplifier; debt burden is near-neutral. The binding constraint on further ROE improvement is operating margin — which as shown in Section 5, faces limited cost-side expansion room — and the underutilisation of leverage.



\### Du Pont ROE vs. Direct ROE — Expected Mismatch



\*\*Direct ROE = 9,413.59 / 36,174.405 = 26.02%\*\*

\*\*Du Pont ROE = 26.44%\*\*

\*\*Gap: +0.42 percentage points\*\*



This discrepancy is expected and is a designed feature of the model, not a formula error. The source is a deliberate time-mismatch across the Du Pont components: `RATIO\_leverage` uses end-of-year FY2025 balances (`BAL\_assets\_total\_2025` = 53,312.36 and `BAL\_equity\_shareholders\_2025` = 34,483.03), while `RATIO\_asset\_turnover` uses the start-of-year `BAL\_assets\_total\_2024` = 55,049.06, and direct ROE uses start-of-year `BAL\_equity\_shareholders\_2024` = 36,174.41.



Because equity declined from FY2024 to FY2025 (from 36,174.41 to 34,483.03 VND bn, a consequence of dividends exceeding net income), the end-of-year leverage ratio of 1.55× is modestly higher than it would be if computed on start-of-year equity. This inflates the Du Pont ROE slightly above the direct figure. The gap of 0.42 percentage points is economically immaterial and directionally correct — it reflects the fact that Vinamilk's equity base shrank during the year.



\### Sustainability Assessment



At 1.55× leverage (essentially 1.0× of financial leverage beyond asset-equity ratio), Vinamilk's ROE is driven almost entirely by operating performance. A leverage ratio of 1.55× means the business funds 35.3% of its assets with liabilities (predominantly current operating liabilities, not financial debt). If Vinamilk were to introduce, for example, 5,000 VND bn in long-term debt at a cost of 7% (approximately the current Vietnam corporate bond rate), the after-tax interest cost would be 5,000 × 7% × 0.80 = 280 VND bn annually, reducing net income modestly, but the equity base would shrink correspondingly, increasing the leverage multiplier. At current NOPAT margins, the ROE enhancement from moderate leverage would be meaningful — roughly 1.5–2.0 percentage points — without approaching distress given TIE already at 32.73×. This is the quantitative case for the leverage optimisation recommendation in Section 9.

\## 9. Strategic Recommendations



\### Recommendation 1 — Resolve the Capital Allocation Contradiction (Near-term: 1–2 years)



With a cash ratio of 1.25× and cash holdings of 23,149.74 VND bn, Vinamilk holds approximately 4,629 VND bn in excess cash above the level needed to cover current liabilities in full (i.e., cash beyond 18,520.29 VND bn). Simultaneously, FY2025 dividends of 11,264.48 VND bn exceeded net income by 1,850.89 VND bn, requiring a drawdown of retained earnings. This combination — holding excess cash while distributing beyond earnings — is internally inconsistent. Management should define and publish an explicit minimum operating cash balance (recommended: 1.0× current liabilities = 18,520 VND bn) and redirect any balance above this threshold toward productive deployment: capacity investment where marginal return exceeds WACC, share buybacks at current market-to-book of 3.95×, or selective M\&A in adjacent dairy or cold-chain categories. Continuing to fund above-earnings dividends from a cash hoard suppresses EVA by keeping capital in idle instruments earning below the 9% WACC.



\### Recommendation 2 — Introduce Strategic Leverage to Amplify ROE (Medium-term: 3–5 years)



Vinamilk's long-term debt of 62.91 VND bn against equity of 34,483.03 VND bn represents a near-total absence of financial leverage — an outlier for a company with TIE of 32.73× and EVA of 6,404.32 VND bn. The cost of this conservatism is a leverage ratio of 1.55×, which caps Du Pont ROE amplification to 0.55 turns above the pure-operating baseline. A targeted capital structure shift — introducing 8,000–12,000 VND bn in long-term debt at Vietnamese corporate bond rates (\~7%) and deploying proceeds into capacity or buybacks — would increase the leverage multiplier toward 1.80–2.0×, lifting Du Pont ROE to approximately 29–32% without moving TIE below 15×. This is appropriate for the HOSE context, where institutional investors increasingly apply capital efficiency screens, and where Vinamilk's current balance sheet appears suboptimally conservative relative to its cash generation capacity.



\### Recommendation 3 — Reduce Days in Inventory to Recover Asset Turnover (Near-term: 1–2 years)



Inventory grew 20.3% in FY2025 (from 5,686.84 to 6,839.28 VND bn) while revenue grew at a lower rate, pushing days in inventory to 58.49 days. For a dairy manufacturer — where raw material freshness is operationally critical and holding costs are real — a 58-day cycle represents excess buffer. A reduction of 10 days in inventory (to approximately 48 days) would release approximately 972 VND bn in working capital (10 days × 97.23 VND bn daily COGS). Redeployed at the current ROA of 17.57%, this releases approximately 171 VND bn in additional NOPAT annually and improves asset turnover toward 1.19×. Management should target the specific SKU categories and raw material procurement lead times driving the inventory build before attributing it entirely to intentional safety-stock policy.



\### Recommendation 4 — Protect Operating Margin Against COGS Creep (Medium-term: 3–5 years)



The operating profit margin of 15.20% is sound but exposed: COGS at 55.76% of sales and SGA at 23.77% of sales together consume 79.5% of revenue before arriving at EBIT. With depreciation embedded in both line items under VAS (and the true depreciation add-back of 1,233.18 VND bn confirming ongoing capital consumption), any input cost inflation — raw milk prices, packaging, energy — passes directly through COGS without a visible depreciation buffer on the income statement. Given that Vietnam's dairy input costs are correlated with global commodity cycles and VND/USD movements, management should pursue a combination of long-term supply contracts for raw milk (hedging volume, if not price), packaging material standardisation, and pricing escalation clauses in distributor agreements. A 1 percentage point compression in operating margin from 15.20% to 14.20% would reduce NOPAT by approximately 636 VND bn and EVA by the same amount — a material erosion given the current EVA base of 6,404 VND bn.



\### Recommendation 5 — Align Dividend Policy with a Sustainable Payout Ratio (Near-term: 1–2 years)



FY2025 dividends of 11,264.48 VND bn represent a payout ratio of 119.6% of net income — structurally unsustainable without either growing earnings or liquidating assets. While Vinamilk's current cash balance absorbs this excess in the near term, the medium-term trajectory is a declining retained earnings base (already down from 15,240.74 to 13,549.36 VND bn in one year) that reduces the book value of equity, compresses total capitalisation, and — if not corrected — will eventually force a dividend cut that the market will interpret as a distress signal, creating disproportionate re-rating risk on a stock currently trading at 3.95× book. Management should establish a sustainable payout ratio in the 80–90% range (approximately 7,530–8,470 VND bn at current net income), communicate it transparently to HOSE investors, and frame the reduction explicitly as a capital reallocation — redirecting the balance toward buybacks or strategic investment — rather than a cash shortage. This framing preserves the market-to-book premium while correcting the structural payout inconsistency.

\## 11. Executive Justification



This analysis was executed strictly from the Stage 4 Technical Specification (`2026-05-24-tran-vinamilk-spec.md`, v1.1) without external data sources, supplementary research, or assumptions beyond those stated in the spec. Every ratio formula references a named range defined in Part A; every interpretation follows the guidance in Part B. The two HIL corrections embedded in the spec — year-suffixed named ranges and the VAS depreciation treatment — were operationally critical: without the `\_2025`/`\_2024` convention, ratio formulas referencing balance sheet items across two years are unresolvable; without the `INC\_depreciation` = 0.00 correction, the cash coverage ratio would collapse to TIE (a silent error producing a plausible but wrong number).



The five strategic recommendations are grounded exclusively in computed ratio values — no external benchmarks beyond those specified in Part B (Vietnamese consumer staples peer ROE of 15–25%, dairy/FMCG market-to-book of 2–5×) have been introduced. The capital allocation contradiction identified in Recommendations 1 and 5 is a direct output of the liquidity and profitability data, not an editorial overlay: when a cash ratio exceeds 1.0× and dividends exceed net income in the same fiscal year, the data compels the conclusion without requiring interpretation.
